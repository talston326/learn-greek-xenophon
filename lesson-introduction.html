Part 1

What is Ancient Greek?

Learn Ancient Greek with Xenophon is a first course in Greek that treats language as a way into character, action, custom, and judgment. Students will build the alphabet, vocabulary, forms, syntax, and reading habits needed for Greek, while returning again and again to Xenophon: soldier, student of Socrates, historian, estate manager, and writer of clear practical prose.

The course will move from the smallest units of reading toward scenes of Ancient Greek life: home, work, family, worship, friendship, leadership, courage, moderation, memory, and public responsibility. Xenophon gives students a world where language and values meet. A word is never merely a word; it belongs to a household, a conversation, a march, a courtroom, a farm, a city, or an argument about how one ought to live.

The Greek Alphabet

Greek has twenty-four letters. Students should learn each letter by sight, name, sound, and example word, then practice the marks that change how a word is read: breathings, accents, diphthongs, iota subscripts, and consonant combinations.

Vowels and Diphthongs

Short and long vowels matter. Common diphthongs include αι, ει, οι, αυ, ευ, ου, υι, ηυ; pronounce them as paired sounds learned by practice.

Breathings

A rough breathing adds an initial h-sound: ὁ. A smooth breathing has no h-sound: ὁδός differs from ὀδός in the mark at the beginning.

Iota Subscript

ᾳ, ῃ, and ῳ preserve an older iota under a long vowel. It affects recognition and history more than normal classroom pronunciation.

Consonant Groups

γ before γ, κ, ξ, or χ sounds like ng. ξ equals κ + σ, ψ equals π + σ, and ζ is commonly taught as σ + δ or dz.

Double Consonants

Double letters are held or articulated distinctly: λλ, μμ, νν, ππ, ττ, and others help students see syllables and word formation.

Accents

Acute, grave, and circumflex accents mark pitch in ancient pronunciation and guide careful reading aloud in this course.
