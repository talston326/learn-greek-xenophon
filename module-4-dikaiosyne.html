Module IV: Δικαιοσύνη —
Justice and the City

The final module centers on δικαιοσύνη, justice. In Greek thought, justice is both personal and political. It concerns the character of the individual, but also the laws, customs, obligations, and shared life of the city. A just person must know how to treat others rightly; a just city must know how to order power, responsibility, reward, punishment, and common purpose.

Xenophon's writings offer many ways to think about justice. In the Memorabilia, Socrates examines what people owe to friends, family, gods, and city. In the Hellenica, Xenophon records the instability and conflict of Greek political life after the Peloponnesian War. In the Cyropaedia, he imagines kingship, authority, and moral education through the figure of Cyrus. These texts ask a question that is still alive: how can human beings live together without letting ambition, fear, faction, or injustice destroy the community?

This module brings together the ethical work of the earlier modules. Wisdom helps us judge; courage helps us act; self-control helps us govern ourselves; justice asks how those virtues are expressed toward others. As students read Xenophon's Greek, they will meet language about law, obligation, friendship, citizenship, command, obedience, and responsibility. The goal is not only to learn forms and vocabulary, but to see how Greek prose can become a training ground for moral and civic reflection.
