Module I: Σοφία —
Wisdom and Socrates

In this opening module, we begin with σοφία, wisdom — not as a possession, but as a practice. For Xenophon, wisdom is closely connected with Socrates, whose conversations teach that learning begins when a person recognizes what he does not yet know. Socratic wisdom is not merely cleverness. It is the habit of asking better questions, examining one's life, testing one's assumptions, and learning to distinguish appearance from reality.

Our readings will draw especially from Xenophon's Memorabilia and related Socratic writings. These texts present Socrates as a teacher, questioner, moral guide, and sometimes a surprisingly practical advisor. Xenophon's Socrates is interested not only in abstract philosophy, but in how a person should live: how to know oneself, how to recognize one's own limits, how to become useful to friends and city, and how to cultivate sound judgment.

As you work through this module, you will begin reading Greek that is connected to real philosophical conversation. The grammar and vocabulary are not separate from the ideas. Each new form you learn will help you approach Xenophon's Greek more directly and see how language carries thought. By the end of this module, σοφία should feel less like a distant philosophical word and more like the beginning of a lifelong discipline: the disciplined love of learning.
