Module II: Ἀνδρεία —
Courage and Leadership

In this module, we turn to ἀνδρεία, courage. In Xenophon, courage is never simply recklessness or love of danger. It is the ability to act with steadiness when fear, hardship, confusion, or danger might otherwise take command. Courage requires endurance, but it also requires judgment. The courageous person must know when to stand firm, when to obey, when to lead, and when restraint is wiser than display.

Xenophon gives us some of the most vivid leadership scenes in Greek literature. In the Anabasis, Greek soldiers find themselves stranded deep in hostile territory after the death of Cyrus. The army survives not because danger disappears, but because leaders emerge who can speak clearly, organize action, and sustain morale. Xenophon's own role in that story gives students a powerful example of courage under pressure.

This module will use Xenophon's military and leadership writings to connect Greek language with action. Students will encounter vocabulary of movement, command, danger, decision, and endurance. But the deeper question is ethical: what kind of person can remain useful and honorable when circumstances become frightening? For Xenophon, courage is not noise or bravado. It is disciplined strength in the service of good judgment.
