Module III: Σωφροσύνη —
Self-Control and Discipline

Σωφροσύνη is one of the most important and difficult Greek virtues to translate. It can mean self-control, moderation, sound-mindedness, discipline, or the ability to govern oneself. In this module, we will treat σωφροσύνη as the virtue of inner order: the capacity to desire rightly, act deliberately, and keep appetite, ambition, anger, and pleasure under the guidance of reason.

Xenophon returns to this virtue again and again. His Socrates insists that no one can be truly free who is ruled by bodily cravings or uncontrolled desires. In the Oeconomicus, Xenophon explores order and discipline through the management of a household. In the Cyropaedia, he presents leaders who must train themselves before they can command others. Self-control, for Xenophon, is not grim denial. It is what makes freedom, friendship, leadership, and learning possible.

This module will invite students to see grammar itself as a form of discipline. Greek asks the reader to attend carefully: to endings, agreement, tense, case, and word order. That attention is part of the intellectual training of the course. As students read about σωφροσύνη, they will also practice it — slowing down, observing closely, and bringing order to complexity.
